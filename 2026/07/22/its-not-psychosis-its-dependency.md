# It's Not Psychosis. It's Dependency.

**Published on:** 2026/07/22

**Tags:** ai, career, private-cloud, local-models, open-knowledge, personal-growth, culture

---

> "I strongly believe there are entire companies right now under heavy AI psychosis and it's impossible to have rational conversations with them about it." — [Mitchell Hashimoto](https://x.com/mitchellh)

I read that line and nodded, the way you nod when someone names a thing you've been feeling but hadn't said out loud. Then I sat with it for a day, because I don't think it's psychosis. Psychosis implies you've lost touch with reality. What I see is closer to the opposite: a lot of these companies were never in touch with their own infrastructure, their own models, or their own judgment in the first place. Hell, not even with their own people, or their own values, because the personnel rotates too fast for anyone to learn what those values were supposed to be before they get lost, or deluded, and rotated out again. And now that the ground is shifting, they have nothing solid to stand on while they figure out what's real. That's not madness. That's what dependency looks like when it finally gets tested.

I didn't get here through a computer science degree and a straight line into a data center, though technology was never far from me either. My father worked for IBM, and he's the one who taught me computing: the old way, over screeching modems, downloading programs off BBS boards, keeping a Novell Netware server alive, reading DOS 3.1 and Windows 3.11 for Workgroups like scripture because there was no vendor support line to call when something broke. But that wasn't the whole education. I sold newspapers on the street as a kid. I was a baker before sunrise. I mixed cement as a construction worker. I was the guy who held the ladder and handed over the paint for a sign painter who never once explained what he was doing, because you learn a trade by watching hands, not by asking questions. The rule underneath all of it, the one I still live by, was simple: work with whatever you can get your hands on now, and later, once you've actually earned the experience, work with what you want. Every one of those jobs (the technical ones and the manual ones) taught me the same lesson, over and over, in different clothes: you get good at something by doing the hard part yourself, badly, for a long time, until it isn't hard anymore. There is no shortcut around that, only people willing to sell you one.

That's the lesson I keep coming back to now that I spend my days working on Private Cloud AI, thinking about where infrastructure should live and who should own it. I believe, plainly, that keeping your infrastructure private, running models locally, and building on open knowledge instead of someone else's black box is how you secure your future, not just your company's, yours personally. Not because "open" and "local" are trendy words. Because the moment you don't understand or control the thing you depend on, you've handed your judgment to whoever does. That's true of a data center. It's just as true of your own mind.

And here's where I think the mania everyone's writing about actually comes from. It's not that AI is too powerful or too useless. It's that most organizations, and a lot of individuals, never built the muscle to evaluate anything on their own terms. So when something new and genuinely capable shows up, they have no fixed point to reason from. They swing between blind faith and panic, sometimes in the same meeting, because faith and panic are the same coin flipped over: both are what outsourced judgment looks like from the outside.

And underneath all of that sits something simpler than lost judgment: greed.

> "OpenAI, Inc. has been transformed into a closed-source de facto subsidiary of the largest technology company in the world: Microsoft. Under its new board, it is not just developing but is actually refining an AGI to maximize profits for Microsoft, rather than for the benefit of humanity." — from Elon Musk's lawsuit against OpenAI, later heard at trial in 2026

Say what you want about who filed that complaint or why. The line still lands, because it names the whole trade in one sentence: a mission for "the benefit of humanity" quietly swapped out for a mission to maximize profit for whoever's holding the equity. That's the pattern, not the exception. The investors funding this didn't see a tool, they saw a machine that turns millions into billions, and they want it running at full speed no matter what it costs somebody else. So the fastest lever gets pulled: cut the people, count that as savings, call the savings "AI budget." Then, because managing a system nobody understands has suddenly become the premium skill of the moment, they hire people who've never touched the domain and pay them more than the experienced staff they just let go, and hand them the keys. A few months later, someone in a meeting asks why the product isn't behaving the way the deck promised. Nobody in the room can answer, because the people who could have answered aren't in the room anymore. That's not a technology failure. That's a decision, made on purpose, by people who valued the multiplier more than the humans doing the multiplying. That's the part of this whole mania that isn't confusion or fear at all. It's just old-fashioned greed wearing a new coat, and it's costing us more humanity than any of us are pricing in.

None of this means I think AI is the enemy. I think it's one of the best learning tools I've ever had access to: a tireless tutor that never gets bored of my questions, that turns a week of dead-end searching into an afternoon of real progress. But a tool that helps you learn and a tool that replaces the learning are not the same thing, and the difference is entirely up to you. Nobody became a baker by watching a machine bake. You still have to put your hands in the dough. If you let AI do all of the doing, you never build the callouses (literal or otherwise) that make the next hard thing easier. Growth has always required friction. That hasn't changed just because the friction got optional.

So when I hear someone say "AI took my job," I understand the fear, I've been afraid of losing income more times than I can count, but I don't think sitting down with that sentence is the answer. Every job, and every technology, I've ever depended on was replaced by the next thing eventually: the newspaper stand by better distribution, the sign painter by vinyl printers, Novell Netware by TCP/IP, DOS by a graphical interface my father was convinced was a waste of memory, and one day probably some part of what I do now by something better than me at it too. The move was never to stop the new thing from arriving. It was to keep evolving faster than it could make me obsolete, and to use the time it freed up to go learn the next thing instead of mourning the last one. The novelty has to find where it fits alongside us. But we also have to keep finding where we fit next to it. That's always been the deal. AI didn't invent it.

None of this is theoretical for me, either. Ten years before any of this was fashionable, I was already building AI for video games: NPCs that had to make decisions, not just play back animations. When I joined Rackspace Technology, I was pitching AI-driven improvements to our ticket system years before "AI hype" was a phrase anyone used, because it solved an actual problem, not because a slide deck told me to. And when this current wave hit for real, I didn't wait around for a mandate from above. I went and found a manager who was already thinking on the same rail I was, and asked to move onto his team so I could work on AI directly. That's the difference I keep coming back to: going toward the thing because you understand what it's actually for, versus getting swept into it because everyone else in the room either went quiet with fear or fell in love with a demo.

Here's the part I don't think gets said enough, though: it's not fair to hand that advice to someone from a position of safety and call it a day. If your work benefits from this shift (and mine does), you owe something back to the people whose work doesn't. Not a motivational quote. Real counsel: here's a plan B, here's a skill that's still yours to build, here's someone who'll actually sit with you and figure out the next move. Somebody has to do that work, deliberately, instead of leaving the displaced to either panic or pretend everything's fine. The people who pulled the cost-cutting lever won't be the ones sitting with someone at their kitchen table figuring out next steps. That was never going to be their job. It falls to the rest of us, and that's not charity. That's just what you do for people when you've been the one picking up the ladder before, and you remember what it felt like to not know what came next.

So, [Mitchell](https://x.com/mitchellh/status/2055380239711457578), you said you worry about how this plays out. I think it plays out fine, for the people and the companies who choose ownership and learning over faith or fear. That choice is available right now. It starts with what infrastructure you actually control, and it continues with what you decide to still do yourself, by hand, even when a model gladly volunteers to do it for you.
